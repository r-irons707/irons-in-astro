I am a Master's student at Queen's University studying astronomy and astrophysics. My research focuses on investigating the influence of magnetic fields on bubble morphology in star-forming regions. Simply: We expect magnetic fields to have a certain shape in regions surrounding massive stars in regions of ionized Hydrogen (called HII regions). My job is to develop methods to effectively (computationally inexpensive and accurately) determine the relationship between magnetic fields and bubbles (cavities of underdensity). This work involves the use of machine learning on polarization maps... More to come!

Master's Research

Magnetic fields play a fundamental role in the process of star formation, influencing the structure and evolution of molecular clouds. One challenge comes from the energy released from massive stars that can alter the magnetic field to align along the edges of expanding bubbles. Despite their importance, the precise role of magnetic fields in bubble evolution remains an open question, and a comprehensive statistical analysis of a large sample of bubbles is necessary in order to work towards understanding this question. Thermal emission from dust grains creates a linear polarization that heralds a circular magnetic field pattern in the plane of sky, which typically falls in line with the edges of a circular bubble. We then expect magnetic field lines to follow tangentially to the bubble, creating a circular pattern. We are applying generalized algorithms to polarization maps to study the impact of these bubbles on magnetic field lines. Our algorithm searches for this expected pattern to locate these bubbles. In doing so, we can determine broader trends in how bubbles influence their environment and establish a clearer relationship between polarization efficiency and bubble properties.

Previous Research Projects

Undergraduate Thesis

Undergraduate thesis on the dynamics of star formation and formation efficiency in the HII regions of NGC 6946. We study NGC 6946, a face-on spiral galaxy located at a distance of 5.5 Mpc. Neutral atomic Hydrogen (HI) and ionized atomic Hydrogen (HII) regions are two key components in the Interstellar Medium (ISM). We introduce these different phases of the ISM with four equilibrium principles that govern them: Kinetic Equilibrium, Excitation Equilibrium, Ionization Equilibrium, and Pressure Equilibrium. Our project focuses on star forming regions and more specifically the star formation efficiency in NGC 6946. The project uses data from the SITELLE instrument at the Canada-France-Hawaii Telescope, which covers Hα and Hβ emission, along with ancillary data of HI and CO emission. We estimate the star formation efficiency in the different HII regions and look for correlations with physical properties at small scale and large scale in the galaxy. We also present analysis on the star formation rate and its distribution throughout the galaxy, along with two methods of measuring the metallicity in the gas and check for consistency between the two results. Final year research project supervised by Dr. Laurie Rousseau-Nepton.

Summer Undergraduate Research Program (SURP) 2023

Dwarf galaxy candidate discovery focused. Worked primarily on discovery of potential candidates in the field of NGC3621 and analysis supervised by Dr. Dae-Sik Moon. We conduct a thorough visual inspection on stacked KMTNet images of the eight sub-fields to identify DGCs around NGC3621. We use the I-band images in this search of DGCs, applying the selection criteria of being a diffuse source extending more than 20 pixels (or ~8 arcsecs) that is absent of any prominent structures (spirals or bars), except for potential nucleated centers. About 35 candidates were confirmed and basic analysis was conducted including fitting the Sersic function to the intensity profiles of the galaxies.
